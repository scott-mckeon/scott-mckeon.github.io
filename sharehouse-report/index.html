The sharehouse is no longer a phase.

Roughly 4.5 million Australians live in share accommodation. We asked 3,562 of them who they are, why they're here and what it's really like. The answers rewrite the national picture of who shares a home, and for how long.

Finding one

The age inversion

Australia still talks about share accommodation as a young person's problem: a uni phase, a first-job stopgap. The data describes something structural. The single largest age group in Australian share accommodation is over 40, long-term sharing measured in decades is normal, and more than one in six residents already own a home, renting rooms to make a mortgage work. When every age group is sharing, sharing is not a life stage. It is part of how the housing system now functions.

Finding four

What it feels like

Living with people you didn't grow up with means sharing costs, chores and decisions with no rulebook. Most houses make it work, but almost everyone carries a story about the time it didn't.

Have paid more than their fair share of bills39.9%
Have chased a housemate for late rent33.1%

Nobody reports these as scandals. They come up the way people talk about weather: unavoidable, mildly wearing, and part of the deal. And still, 86.1% describe their household as a positive place to live.

Deep dive · energy and utilities

The bills nobody owns

Electricity is the most-shared bill in Australian sharehouses, yet shared bills belong to everyone and no one. Regulators keep cutting benchmark prices and mandating better offers, but a deal only helps the household that goes looking for it. Year after year of research says most households simply don't. These numbers aren't going down on their own - which is why we built Homerun Switch: set your house up once, and we watch the market, remind you when it's worth moving, and handle the switch from there.

The numbers behind the numbers

Why this is happening

  • National rents have risen 42.9% in five years, roughly $204 a week on the median rental (Cotality, formerly CoreLogic).
  • The national median dwelling value passed $940,000 in May 2026. PropTrack rates affordability the worst since its records began in 1995.
  • The average first home buyer is now around 36. Home ownership has fallen to 36% among 25–29 year olds.
  • Rental vacancy sits near 1.7% nationally, against a pre-pandemic norm around 3.3%.
  • Flatmates.com.au's national survey found 48% share primarily because they can't afford to live alone, with over-55s the fastest growing group on the platform.
